When you trace the map of Acts, two birthplaces face each other across the northeastern corner of the Mediterranean: the island of **Cyprus**, where Joseph the Levite was born, and **Tarsus of Cilicia**, where Saul was born. The two men never knew, growing up within sight of the same sea, that God was preparing each of them for the other — and both of them for the nations.

## Barnabas: the Son of Encouragement from Cyprus

Joseph was born on Cyprus, an island with a large and prosperous Jewish community that had been rooted there since the days of the Ptolemies. He was a **Levite** (Acts 4:36) — a man of the tribe set apart to serve the temple, educated in the Scriptures, trained in the worship of Israel. And he was a **Hellenistic Jew** — at home in the Greek language, at ease in the cosmopolitan cities of the Roman world. Few men in the early church carried both worlds inside them the way he did: the credibility of Jerusalem and the fluency of the diaspora.

We meet him first not by his preaching but by his giving. He sold a field that belonged to him and laid the money at the apostles' feet (Acts 4:37) — freely, joyfully, without conditions. Luke sets his honest generosity directly against the deceit of Ananias and Sapphira in the next chapter (Acts 5:1–11): one man gave everything openly; a couple pretended to give everything and lied to the Holy Spirit. The contrast is the lesson. The new community of the Kingdom has room for the poor and room for the rich, but no room for the mask.

The apostles gave Joseph a new name: **Barnabas, "Son of Encouragement"** (Acts 4:36). In Scripture a name given by spiritual authority is a recognition of character. His whole life proves the name. Through the persecution that followed Stephen's death, through the terror of Saul's raids on the church, Barnabas remained what he was: a good man, full of the Holy Spirit and of faith (Acts 11:24).

## Saul: the Pharisee from Tarsus

Saul was born in **Tarsus of Cilicia** — "a citizen of no ordinary city," as he himself said (Acts 21:39), and a Roman citizen from birth (Acts 22:28). Tarsus was a center of learning and trade, and Saul carried its sophistication with him to Jerusalem, where he was trained under Gamaliel in the strictest tradition of the Pharisees (Acts 22:3). His zeal made him the church's fiercest enemy: he consented to Stephen's death and dragged believers to prison (Acts 8:1–3).

Then the risen Christ met him on the Damascus road (Acts 9:1–19), and the persecutor became a preacher. But conversion did not mean instant acceptance. When Saul came to Jerusalem, the disciples were afraid of him and did not believe he was a disciple (Acts 9:26). The man who would write half the New Testament stood outside the door of the church — until Barnabas opened it.

## The courage to vouch for a dangerous man

**"But Barnabas took him and brought him to the apostles"** (Acts 9:27). That one sentence may be among the most consequential in church history. Barnabas staked his own reputation on the reality of Saul's conversion, told the apostles what Christ had done on the road, and won the persecutor a place among the persecuted. Encouragement is not a soft gift. It took more courage to stand beside Saul than it took to flee from him.

When the plots against Saul's life grew, the brothers sent him home to Tarsus (Acts 9:30) — and there he waited, in obscurity, for years. God's preparation often includes a hidden season. The map remembers what we forget: between Damascus and Antioch stands Tarsus, the place of waiting.

## Antioch: where the two lives meet

The believers scattered by persecution carried the word to Phoenicia, Cyprus, and Antioch — and there, men of Cyprus and Cyrene began preaching the Lord Jesus to the Greeks also, and a great number believed (Acts 11:19–21). When the news reached Jerusalem, the church sent their most trusted bridge-builder. Barnabas, the Cypriot Levite, arrived in Antioch, **saw the grace of God, and was glad** (Acts 11:23). He did not demand that the Gentiles become Jews. He recognized God's work and encouraged them to remain true to the Lord.

Then he did something only a man free of ambition could do: he left a thriving revival to go find a forgotten man. Barnabas went to **Tarsus to look for Saul** (Acts 11:25), brought him to Antioch, and for a whole year the two taught great numbers together. It was there that the disciples were first called **Christians** (Acts 11:26). And when famine threatened Judea, the Gentile church sent relief to their Jewish brothers by the hands of Barnabas and Saul (Acts 11:29–30) — the daughter church feeding the mother church, grace flowing back along the road it came.

## Sent out together — and the hard mercy of separation

At Antioch the Holy Spirit said, "Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them" (Acts 13:2). Their first journey began on **Cyprus — Barnabas's home island** — preaching from Salamis to Paphos, then on through Pisidia and Lycaonia. Along the way the order of the names quietly reverses: "Barnabas and Saul" becomes "Paul and Barnabas." At Lystra the crowds called Barnabas *Zeus* and Paul *Hermes*, "because he was the chief speaker" (Acts 14:12). Barnabas had done what true fathers in the faith do — raised up a son who outgrew him, and rejoiced.

Their one recorded conflict was over a person, not a doctrine. Paul refused to take John Mark, who had deserted them; Barnabas — Mark's cousin, and forever the encourager — refused to leave him behind (Acts 15:36–39). The disagreement was sharp, and they parted: Paul took Silas through Syria, and Barnabas took Mark and **sailed home to Cyprus**. God folded even this sorrow into His purpose — two teams went out instead of one. And years later Paul himself wrote, "Get Mark and bring him with you, for he is very useful to me for ministry" (2 Timothy 4:11). Barnabas's patience with a failed young man gave the church the author of the second Gospel.

## What the two birthplaces teach us

Stand back and look at the map. From **Cyprus** came the encourager; from **Tarsus** came the apostle; at **Antioch** God joined them, and from Antioch the gospel went out to the ends of the earth. Neither man's story works without the other. Paul needed someone to believe in him before anyone else would; Barnabas needed someone worth believing in to spend his life on. The church still runs on both kinds of men — the brilliant and the generous, the voice and the bridge — and the Spirit who set them apart together is still calling both.

*Tradition holds that Barnabas returned to Cyprus and was martyred at Salamis around AD 61 — buried by John Mark on the island where he was born. The encourager's road ended where it began, and his legacy lives in every life he refused to give up on.*
